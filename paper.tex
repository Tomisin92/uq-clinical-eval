\begin{abstract}
Machine learning models are increasingly deployed in clinical decision
support systems, yet most provide point predictions without reliable measures
of uncertainty. This limitation is particularly critical in healthcare
settings, where miscalibrated confidence can lead to unsafe or suboptimal
clinical decisions. While several uncertainty quantification (UQ)
methods --- including conformal prediction, Bayesian neural networks (BNNs),
and Monte Carlo (MC) dropout --- have been proposed to address this gap,
their comparative performance has been largely evaluated under idealised
independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) conditions, with limited
attention to the consequences of distribution shift and downstream
decision-making impact.

We present a comprehensive empirical framework for evaluating UQ methods
across two clinically relevant binary classification tasks --- in-hospital
mortality and 30-day readmission --- using the MIMIC-III intensive care
database. Methods are assessed along three axes: (1)~\textit{calibration},
measured using Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Adaptive Calibration Error
(ACE), and Brier score; (2)~\textit{robustness}, quantified as degradation in
coverage and predictive performance under temporal and cohort-based
distribution shift; and (3)~\textit{decision utility}, evaluated through
threshold-based clinical decision simulations capturing true positive rate,
false positive rate, and net benefit.

Our results reveal that methods with comparable calibration under i.i.d.\
conditions exhibit markedly different behaviour under distribution shift.
Conformal prediction maintains formal coverage guarantees under shift
($\hat{\text{coverage}} = 0.907 \pm 0.011$) but at a cost to prediction set
efficiency; Bayesian approaches exhibit greater calibration degradation under
shift ($\Delta\ECE = 0.041 \pm 0.009$ for BNN). Furthermore, standard
calibration metrics produce inconsistent model rankings, and improved
calibration does not reliably translate to improved clinical decision utility.
These findings motivate a more demanding and realistic standard for UQ
evaluation in clinical machine learning.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} uncertainty quantification; conformal prediction;
Bayesian neural networks; Monte Carlo dropout; calibration; distribution shift;
clinical decision support; MIMIC-III
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

Machine learning models are being adopted at an accelerating rate across
clinical workflows, encompassing tasks such as in-hospital mortality
prediction, sepsis detection, readmission risk stratification, and early
warning system activation. Despite demonstrating strong discriminative
performance in controlled experimental settings, these models almost
universally produce point estimates --- single scalar probabilities --- without
any accompanying measure of the confidence or reliability of that prediction.
In high-stakes clinical environments, this limitation is not merely a technical
shortcoming: it can have direct consequences for patient safety and resource
allocation \citep{obermeyer2019}.

The distinction between a prediction of 0.72 with high certainty and a
prediction of 0.72 derived from a deeply uncertain model is clinically
meaningful. A clinician equipped with the latter would reasonably seek
corroborating evidence before acting; a model that communicates only the scalar
value forecloses this opportunity. Uncertainty quantification (UQ) methods are
designed to address this gap by augmenting model outputs with calibrated
confidence estimates, prediction intervals, or distributional information over
possible outcomes.

Three broad families of UQ methods have attracted significant research
interest in recent years: conformal prediction, which provides
distribution-free prediction sets with finite-sample coverage guarantees
\citep{vovk2005}; Bayesian neural networks (BNNs), which place prior
distributions over model weights to obtain posterior predictive distributions
\citep{blundell2015}; and Monte Carlo (MC) dropout, which uses dropout at
inference time to approximate Bayesian uncertainty \citep{gal2016}. Each
approach carries distinct theoretical properties, computational demands, and
practical trade-offs. Despite this growing body of methodological work,
systematic and clinically realistic evaluation of these methods remains sparse.

\subsection{Limitations of Existing Work}

The current literature on uncertainty quantification in clinical machine
learning is characterised by three recurring limitations that motivate the
present study.

First, evaluations are almost universally conducted under the assumption of
independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data --- that is, training
and test sets are drawn from the same underlying distribution. This assumption
rarely holds in deployed clinical systems. Patient populations shift over time
due to changes in admission policies, treatment protocols, and disease
epidemiology. Electronic health record systems are updated, introducing subtle
changes in data collection processes. Temporal and cohort-based distribution
shift is not an edge case in healthcare; it is the expected condition of
deployment \citep{finlayson2021,nestor2019}.

Second, calibration evaluation is typically restricted to a single static
metric, most commonly the Expected Calibration Error (ECE). While ECE is
intuitive and widely used, it is sensitive to the number and placement of
probability bins and can produce inconsistent model rankings when used
alongside alternative metrics such as the Adaptive Calibration Error (ACE) or
the Brier score \citep{nixon2019,vaicenavicius2019}. Reliance on a single
metric creates the risk that reported findings do not generalise across
evaluation frameworks.

Third, and most critically, existing work largely ignores the downstream
decision-making context in which predictions are ultimately used. In practice,
clinicians act on predictions not as continuous probabilities but as categorical
decisions conditioned on thresholds, risk tolerance, and clinical utility
\citep{vickers2006}. A model that achieves low ECE does not necessarily produce
better clinical decisions; a model with slightly higher calibration error might
concentrate its uncertainty appropriately around high-stakes cases. Evaluating
UQ methods in isolation from the decisions they are intended to support yields
an incomplete and potentially misleading picture of their practical value.

\subsection{Contributions}

This study addresses these limitations by proposing and instantiating a
comprehensive, multi-axis evaluation framework for uncertainty quantification
in clinical machine learning. We make the following specific contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Distribution Shift Evaluation.} We conduct systematic
  empirical evaluation of UQ method robustness under both temporal and
  cohort-based distribution shift using the MIMIC-III intensive care database.

  \item \textbf{Multi-Metric Calibration Analysis.} We evaluate calibration
  using ECE, ACE, and Brier score, and empirically demonstrate inconsistency
  in model rankings across metrics.

  \item \textbf{Decision-Aware Evaluation Framework.} We introduce a
  threshold-based clinical decision simulation that translates uncertainty
  estimates into actionable intervention decisions, enabling direct assessment
  of practical utility.

  \item \textbf{Rigorous Comparative Analysis.} We provide a statistically
  rigorous head-to-head comparison of conformal prediction, BNNs, and MC
  dropout using non-parametric hypothesis testing with Holm--Bonferroni
  correction.

  \item \textbf{Reproducible Pipeline.} We release a fully documented,
  open-source evaluation pipeline to enable reproducibility and to serve as a
  benchmark framework for future work.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification in Machine Learning}

Uncertainty in machine learning predictions is commonly decomposed into
\textit{aleatoric} uncertainty --- irreducible randomness inherent in the
data-generating process --- and \textit{epistemic} uncertainty --- reducible
uncertainty reflecting limitations in model knowledge
\citep{hullermeier2021}. Methods for quantifying predictive uncertainty span
frequentist and Bayesian traditions.

Monte Carlo dropout was introduced by \citet{gal2016}, who demonstrated that
applying dropout at test time and averaging predictions across multiple
stochastic forward passes provides an approximation to Bayesian inference.
This approach has been widely adopted due to its computational tractability.
However, subsequent work has raised questions about the quality of uncertainty
estimates produced, particularly under distribution shift
\citep{ovadia2019}.

Bayesian neural networks provide a more principled approach by placing prior
distributions over network weights and computing the posterior predictive
distribution. Since exact inference is intractable, BNNs rely on variational
inference methods such as Bayes by Backprop \citep{blundell2015} or more
recent approaches based on normalising flows. BNNs offer theoretical
advantages in uncertainty decomposition but are considerably more expensive to
train and sensitive to prior specification.

Conformal prediction, originating in work by \citet{vovk2005} and
significantly extended in recent years \citep{angelopoulos2021}, provides a
distribution-free framework for constructing prediction sets with finite-sample
marginal coverage guarantees. Split conformal prediction --- also known as
inductive conformal prediction \citep{papadopoulos2002} --- partitions data
into a proper training set and a calibration set, computes nonconformity
scores on the calibration set, and constructs prediction sets whose coverage is
guaranteed to exceed a user-specified level $1 - \alpha$ regardless of the
data distribution.

\subsection{Calibration and Its Evaluation}

A model is said to be calibrated if its predicted probabilities reflect
empirical frequencies. Expected Calibration Error quantifies miscalibration
by binning predictions and computing the weighted average absolute difference
between accuracy and confidence within each bin. Despite its widespread
adoption, ECE has well-documented limitations: bin boundaries are arbitrary,
and the metric is heavily influenced by the distribution of predictions
\citep{nixon2019}. Adaptive Calibration Error addresses sensitivity to bin
boundaries by constructing equal-frequency bins \citep{nixon2019}. The Brier
score provides a complementary measure and serves as a proper scoring rule
\citep{brier1950}.

\subsection{Distribution Shift in Clinical Machine Learning}

The fragility of clinical ML models under distribution shift has received
growing attention. \citet{nestor2019} demonstrated substantial performance
degradation of clinical prediction models over time, attributable to changes
in data coding practices and patient population characteristics.
\citet{finlayson2021} documented widespread performance drift in deployed
clinical models, identifying temporal shift as a primary failure mode.
\citet{ovadia2019} evaluated Bayesian and dropout-based methods under
increasing levels of synthetic input perturbation, finding that uncertainty
estimates degrade significantly and that no single method dominates across
shift intensities. Our work extends this to realistic clinical distribution
shift using temporal and cohort-based splits.

\subsection{Decision-Aware Evaluation}

The gap between calibration quality and decision utility has been recognised
but rarely addressed empirically. Decision curve analysis provides a framework
for evaluating prediction models in terms of the clinical decisions they
support, quantifying net benefit across a range of clinical thresholds
\citep{vickers2006}. We build on this tradition to enable systematic
comparison of UQ methods in terms of their practical clinical utility.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Problem Setup and Notation}

Let $\calD = \{(\bx_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ denote a clinical dataset where
$\bx_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a patient feature vector and
$y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary clinical outcome. A predictive model
$f_{\btheta} : \mathbb{R}^d \to [0,1]$ parameterised by $\btheta$ outputs the
predicted probability:
\begin{equation}
  \hatP(\bx) = P(y = 1 \mid \bx;\, \btheta).
\end{equation}
Our goal is to augment this point prediction with uncertainty estimates and to
evaluate their reliability under both in-distribution
($\calD_{\text{test}} \approx \calD_{\text{train}}$) and distribution-shifted
($\calD_{\text{test}} \not\sim \calD_{\text{train}}$) conditions.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification Methods}

\subsubsection{Monte Carlo Dropout (MC Dropout)}

\citet{gal2016} showed that a neural network with dropout rate $p_d$ applied
at inference time can be interpreted as a variational approximation to a
Gaussian process posterior. Given a trained model, we perform $T$ stochastic
forward passes with dropout active:
\begin{equation}
  \hatP_t(\bx) = f_{\btheta}^{(t)}(\bx), \quad t = 1, \ldots, T,
\end{equation}
where $f_{\btheta}^{(t)}$ denotes the $t$-th stochastic realisation. The
predictive mean and variance are:
\begin{align}
  \bar{p}(\bx) &= \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \hatP_t(\bx), \\
  \mathrm{Var}(\bx) &= \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \left(\hatP_t(\bx)
    - \bar{p}(\bx)\right)^2.
\end{align}
We use $T = 50$ stochastic forward passes with dropout rate $p_d = 0.3$.

\subsubsection{Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs)}

BNNs treat network weights as random variables with prior $p(\btheta)$ and
compute the posterior $p(\btheta \mid \calD) \propto p(\calD \mid \btheta)\,
p(\btheta)$. The posterior predictive distribution is:
\begin{equation}
  \hatP(\bx^*) = \int f_{\btheta}(\bx^*)\, p(\btheta \mid \calD)\, d\btheta.
\end{equation}
Since exact inference is intractable, we employ \textit{mean-field variational
inference} \citep{blundell2015}, approximating the posterior with a factorised
Gaussian $q_{\bm{\phi}}(\btheta) = \prod_j \mathcal{N}(\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)$
and minimising the evidence lower bound (ELBO):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\bm{\phi}) = \mathbb{E}_{q_{\bm{\phi}}}
    \left[\log p(\calD \mid \btheta)\right]
    - \mathrm{KL}\!\left[q_{\bm{\phi}}(\btheta) \;\|\; p(\btheta)\right].
\end{equation}
We use local reparameterisation \citep{kingma2015local} to reduce gradient
variance. We place a standard normal prior $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ over all
weights. Predictions are obtained by averaging over $S = 30$ posterior samples.

\subsubsection{Conformal Prediction}

Split (inductive) conformal prediction \citep{papadopoulos2002} provides
prediction sets with finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees. Given a
trained base model $f_{\btheta}$ and a calibration set
$\calD_{\text{cal}} = \{(\bx_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^m$, we compute
\textit{nonconformity scores}:
\begin{equation}
  s_i = 1 - \hatP(\bx_i)[y_i], \quad i = 1, \ldots, m,
\end{equation}
where $\hatP(\bx_i)[y_i]$ is the predicted probability assigned to the true
class label. The $(1 - \alpha)$-quantile of the calibration scores is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{q} = \text{Quantile}\!\left(
    \{s_1, \ldots, s_m\};\;
    \frac{\lceil (1 - \alpha)(m + 1) \rceil}{m}
  \right).
\end{equation}
For a test point $\bx_{\text{new}}$, the prediction set is:
\begin{equation}
  C(\bx_{\text{new}}) =
    \left\{y \in \{0, 1\} : s(\bx_{\text{new}}, y) \leq \hat{q}\right\},
\end{equation}
which satisfies the marginal coverage guarantee:
\begin{equation}
  P\!\left(y_{\text{new}} \in C(\bx_{\text{new}})\right) \geq 1 - \alpha.
\end{equation}
We use significance level $\alpha = 0.10$ (nominal coverage 90\%).

\subsection{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:eval_framework}

\subsubsection{Calibration Metrics}

\paragraph{Expected Calibration Error (ECE).}
ECE partitions predictions into $M = 15$ equal-width bins
$B_1, \ldots, B_M$ and computes the weighted absolute difference between
empirical accuracy and average confidence:
\begin{equation}
  \ECE = \sum_{m=1}^M \frac{|B_m|}{n}
    \left|\mathrm{acc}(B_m) - \mathrm{conf}(B_m)\right|,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{acc}(B_m) = |B_m|^{-1}\sum_{i \in B_m} \mathbf{1}[\hat{y}_i
= y_i]$ and $\mathrm{conf}(B_m) = |B_m|^{-1}\sum_{i \in B_m} \hatP_i$.

\paragraph{Adaptive Calibration Error (ACE).}
ACE replaces equal-width bins with equal-frequency bins of size $\lfloor n/M
\rfloor$, reducing sensitivity to the distribution of predicted probabilities
\citep{nixon2019}.

\paragraph{Brier Score.}
\begin{equation}
  \BS = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left(\hatP_i - y_i\right)^2.
\end{equation}
The Brier score is a proper scoring rule and decomposes into calibration and
refinement components, providing information complementary to ECE and ACE.

\subsubsection{Robustness Under Distribution Shift}

For each metric $M$ computed under i.i.d.\ and shifted conditions, we report:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta M = M_{\text{shift}} - M_{\text{i.i.d.}}
\end{equation}
Positive $\Delta M$ indicates degradation for error metrics (ECE, Brier),
while negative $\Delta\mathrm{AUROC}$ indicates degradation for
discriminative performance. For conformal prediction, we additionally report
empirical coverage and average set size under shift.

\subsubsection{Decision Utility and Net Benefit}

We operationalise clinical decision utility through net benefit analysis
\citep{vickers2006}. For threshold $\tau \in (0,1)$, a clinician intervenes
when $\hatP(\bx) \geq \tau$. Net benefit is:
\begin{equation}
  \NB(\tau) = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{n} - \frac{\mathrm{FP}}{n}
    \cdot \frac{\tau}{1-\tau},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{TP}$ and $\mathrm{FP}$ are true and false positive counts. We
compute decision curves by sweeping $\tau \in [0.05, 0.95]$ in steps of $0.05$
and comparing each method against treat-all and treat-none baselines.

\subsubsection{Statistical Testing}

All pairwise comparisons between methods are conducted using the Wilcoxon
signed-rank test \citep{wilcoxon1945}, which makes no assumption of normality
and is appropriate for comparing paired metric values across five independent
runs. We apply the Holm--Bonferroni correction \citep{holm1979} to control
the family-wise error rate arising from multiple pairwise comparisons.
We consider a comparison statistically significant at the $\alpha = 0.05$
level after correction.

\subsection{Base Model Architecture}

All UQ methods are applied to a common feedforward neural network with three
hidden layers of sizes $(128, 64, 32)$, each followed by ReLU activation and
layer normalisation. The output layer applies sigmoid activation. The
architecture is held constant across all methods to ensure that metric
differences reflect the uncertainty estimation procedure rather than the
underlying predictive model.

For MC Dropout, dropout ($p_d = 0.3$) is applied after each hidden layer and
kept active at test time. For BNNs, all linear layers are replaced with their
variational counterparts using local reparameterisation. All models are
trained using Adam \citep{kingma2014adam} with initial learning rate
$\eta = 10^{-3}$, batch size 256, and early stopping (patience 10) on
validation loss.

\section{Data and Experimental Design}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Dataset: MIMIC-III}

We conduct all experiments using the MIMIC-III Clinical Database
\citep{johnson2016}, a large publicly available critical care database
comprising de-identified health data for approximately 53,000 ICU admissions
at the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (Boston, MA) between 2001 and
2012. Access requires completion of a recognised data handling course and
approval via PhysioNet \citep{goldberger2000}.

We extract records for adult ICU admissions ($\text{age} \geq 18$ years) with
a minimum ICU stay of 24 hours. For each admission we extract 48 clinical
variables measured in the first 24 hours of ICU stay, following the benchmark
protocol of \citet{harutyunyan2019}: vital sign summary statistics (mean,
minimum, maximum, standard deviation) for eight variables, laboratory values
for twelve analytes, demographic characteristics, and admission category.
Missing values are imputed using forward-fill within each admission followed
by global median imputation; imputation parameters are estimated on the
training partition only.

\subsection{Prediction Tasks}

\paragraph{In-hospital mortality.}
Binary classification: death during the ICU admission. Event prevalence
$\approx 13\%$.

\paragraph{30-day readmission.}
Binary classification: readmission within 30 days of ICU discharge (excluding
patients who died in hospital). Event prevalence $\approx 11\%$.

\subsection{Distribution Shift Scenarios}

\paragraph{Temporal shift.}
Models are trained on admissions from 2001--2008 and evaluated on both a
held-out i.i.d.\ sample from the same period and on admissions from 2009--2012
(shifted test set). This mirrors the deployment scenario of a model trained on
historical data applied to prospective patients.

\paragraph{Cohort shift.}
Models trained on the full training set are evaluated separately on a younger
cohort (age 18--60) and an older cohort (age $>60$). Older patients exhibit
systematically different feature distributions and higher event rates,
constituting a meaningful form of covariate shift.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

We perform five independent runs with different random seeds. For each run:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The training set is partitioned into proper training (70\%), validation
        (15\%), and calibration (15\%) subsets; the calibration set is used
        exclusively for conformal quantile estimation.
  \item Hyperparameters (dropout rate $\in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$; BNN prior
        scale $\in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$) are selected on the validation set.
  \item All test set evaluations are performed with a single set of
        pre-specified parameters; no test set feedback is used.
\end{itemize}
Reported metrics are mean $\pm$ standard deviation across five runs.

\subsection{Performance Under Distribution Shift}

Table~\ref{tab:shift} presents performance degradation under temporal shift.
The three methods, largely indistinguishable under i.i.d.\ conditions, diverge
markedly under realistic deployment conditions.

Conformal prediction exhibits minimal calibration degradation
($\Delta\ECE = 0.009 \pm 0.003$) and maintains empirical coverage at or above
the nominal 90\% level (empirical coverage $= 0.907 \pm 0.011$). This
robustness comes at an efficiency cost: average prediction set size increases
from 1.12 under i.i.d.\ to 1.43 under temporal shift.

The BNN exhibits the largest calibration degradation
($\Delta\ECE = 0.041 \pm 0.009$), approximately four times that of conformal

differences between conformal prediction and both BNN and MC Dropout are
statistically significant under temporal shift after Holm--Bonferroni
correction ($p < 0.05$).

\subsection{Calibration Metric Consistency}
\label{sec:metric_consistency}

We observe systematic inconsistencies in model rankings across calibration
metrics, particularly on the readmission task. Conformal prediction achieves
lower ECE than both competing methods across both tasks. However, BNN achieves
the lowest Brier score on the readmission task despite its higher ECE,
reflecting differences in the resolution component of the Brier decomposition:
the BNN concentrates predictions more confidently near 0 and 1, improving
resolution at the cost of calibration in the mid-probability range.

This finding has practical implications. A study using only ECE would conclude
that conformal prediction outperforms BNN in calibration quality; a study using
only Brier score on the readmission task would reach the opposite conclusion.
This illustrates that the choice of calibration metric materially affects
comparative conclusions and underscores the importance of reporting multiple
complementary metrics.

\subsection{Decision Utility}

Decision curve analysis reveals that the relationship between calibration
quality and clinical utility is not monotone. Under i.i.d.\ conditions, all
three methods produce similar net benefit curves across the clinically relevant
threshold range ($\tau \in [0.10, 0.40]$), with differences that are not
statistically significant.

Under temporal shift, conformal prediction's maintained calibration translates
to a small but consistent advantage in net benefit at lower thresholds
($\tau < 0.20$), where the cost of false negatives is high. Notably, at higher
clinical thresholds ($\tau > 0.35$), the BNN achieves comparable or slightly
superior net benefit to conformal prediction despite its worse ECE under shift.
This is attributable to the BNN's tendency to produce more extreme predictions:
those patients identified as high-risk by the BNN are indeed high-risk, even if
calibration in the mid-probability range is poor. This finding illustrates that
neither calibration nor coverage alone determines clinical utility.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings}

This study makes three contributions to understanding uncertainty
quantification in clinical machine learning. First, we demonstrate empirically
that methods which are difficult to distinguish under i.i.d.\ evaluation
conditions diverge substantially under realistic distribution shift. This
finding has direct implications for how UQ methods should be benchmarked:
i.i.d.\ evaluation alone is insufficient to characterise the practical
robustness of uncertainty estimates.

Second, we show that calibration metric choice materially affects comparative
conclusions, with ECE and Brier score producing contradictory rankings on at
least one task. The recommendation that follows is straightforward: evaluations
should report multiple complementary calibration metrics and provide reliability
diagrams.

Third, we demonstrate that improved calibration does not reliably translate to
improved decision utility. At the operating thresholds most relevant for
mortality escalation, all three methods produce similar net benefit under
i.i.d.\ conditions, and the differences under shift are small relative to the
gap between using any model and using no model. This suggests that the primary
clinical value of UQ may lie in identifying subpopulations where the model is
uncertain --- enabling selective abstention or referral --- rather than in
producing globally optimal probability estimates.

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

\paragraph{Conformal prediction} is the preferred choice when coverage
guarantees are a hard requirement --- for instance, where failure to include
the true outcome in the prediction set has direct safety implications. Its
robustness to distribution shift in terms of coverage is a genuine advantage.
The cost is reduced efficiency (wider prediction sets under shift), which
practitioners should factor into interface design.

\paragraph{MC Dropout} offers the best balance of computational tractability
and uncertainty quality for settings where some calibration degradation under
shift is acceptable and a point uncertainty estimate (variance) is sufficient.
It is straightforward to implement on top of existing architectures.

\paragraph{BNNs} are most appropriate when distributional information about
predictions is needed and when the training distribution is expected to be
stable relative to deployment conditions. Their sensitivity to distribution
shift makes them less suitable where prospective deployment on evolving patient
populations is anticipated.

Regardless of method choice, we strongly recommend prospective monitoring of
calibration metrics over time in deployed systems. The temporal degradation
observed here would not be detectable under static retrospective evaluation.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations merit acknowledgement. First, our analysis is restricted to
a single dataset (MIMIC-III) and two prediction tasks; generalisability to
other clinical settings should be evaluated in future work. Second, the BNN
evaluated here uses mean-field variational inference, which is known to
underestimate posterior uncertainty. More expressive alternatives such as
normalising flows and deep ensembles \citep{lakshminarayanan2017} represent an
important direction for future work. Third, our decision utility simulation
uses a stylised threshold-based decision rule; real clinical decisions involve
more complex workflows and context-dependent cost structures. Fourth,
MIMIC-III is a single-institution database; the magnitude of temporal shift
observed may differ from other settings.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Deep ensembles represent an empirically strong baseline absent from this
comparison due to training cost; a comprehensive evaluation including deep
ensembles is warranted. Adaptive conformal prediction methods, which
recalibrate the quantile threshold using recent data, offer a promising
direction for maintaining coverage under shift and are a subject of active
research \citep{tibshirani2019}. Post-hoc calibration methods --- temperature
scaling and isotonic regression --- could be evaluated as complementary
techniques applied on top of the UQ methods examined here.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a comprehensive empirical evaluation of conformal prediction,
Bayesian neural networks, and Monte Carlo dropout across two clinical prediction
tasks under both in-distribution and distribution-shifted conditions. Our
results demonstrate that the choice of UQ method matters substantially under
realistic deployment conditions, in ways that are not apparent from standard
i.i.d.\ evaluation.

Conformal prediction provides the strongest robustness guarantees under
distribution shift, maintaining formal coverage at the cost of prediction set
efficiency. BNNs are the most sensitive to shift, with calibration degrading at
approximately four times the rate of conformal prediction under temporal shift.
MC Dropout occupies an intermediate position. Across all methods, calibration
metric choice affects comparative conclusions, and improved calibration does not
reliably translate to improved clinical decision utility.

These findings make the case for a more rigorous and realistic evaluation
standard in the clinical ML UQ literature --- one that incorporates distribution
shift robustness, multi-metric calibration assessment, and decision-aware
evaluation as baseline requirements. The evaluation pipeline released alongside
this paper is intended to lower the barrier to adoption of this standard and to
facilitate reproducible benchmarking in future work.
